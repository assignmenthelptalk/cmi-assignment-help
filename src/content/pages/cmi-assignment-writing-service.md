### Management Reports, Essays, and Reflective Accounts: All CMI Formats

**Management report**: The most common CMI assignment format, particularly at Level 5 and Level 6. A management report includes a title page, executive summary, contents page, body sections divided by the assessment criteria, and a bibliography. Level 5 units such as 502 and 504 typically require this format for their Analyse and Evaluate tasks. We structure every management report to the format specification in the unit brief.

**Essay**: Argumentative or discursive essay format is most common at Level 3 and Level 4, particularly for units that use the command verb Discuss or Analyse. CMI essays differ from university essays in that they are expected to draw on workplace application and management practice, not just academic theory. We apply this distinction throughout.

**Reflective account**: A first-person reflective response using a recognised reflective model, most commonly Gibbs' Reflective Cycle or Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle. Reflective accounts appear across Level 5 and Level 7 CPD-related units. They require the student's (or writer's) perspective on a management experience, structured through the reflective framework to a specific learning outcome.

**Case study analysis**: Applied to a real or hypothetical organisational context, required in Level 6 and Level 7 strategic units. The writer analyses the case against relevant management frameworks and produces recommendations backed by evidence and cited sources.

**Action plan / work-based project**: Required in some applied units across Levels 5–7. These link personal or organisational development goals to CMI Learning Outcomes with measurable targets and timeframes.

### Harvard Referencing and Command Verb Alignment Included

Every assignment is written with Harvard referencing as standard. Sources include ManagementDirect (CMI's own resource platform), peer-reviewed academic journals, CMI publications and research reports, and government or industry data where appropriate. A standard 3,000-word assignment includes a minimum of 8–12 referenced sources, with in-text citations and a full bibliography.

Command verb alignment is the single most common point of failure in CMI assignments. "Analyse" requires breaking a concept into its components and examining the relationship between them; it does not mean describe. "Evaluate" requires making a reasoned judgement supported by evidence; it does not mean list advantages and disadvantages. "Justify" requires a defended position with evidence-backed reasoning; it does not mean explain.

Every assignment we write applies the command verb at the cognitive depth the assessor expects. This is not a minor stylistic distinction. It is the difference between a Distinction and a referral.

## How Do We Ensure Your Assignment Meets Your Specific CMI Unit Requirements
Every CMI unit has its own Learning Outcomes, Assessment Criteria, and command verb specification. An assignment that meets the generic definition of "a management report" does not automatically meet the criteria for Unit 502 or Unit 701. Each unit has specific points of evidence the assessor is looking for.

We ensure unit-specific alignment by working directly from the assignment task you provide. When you send your unit brief or assignment question on WhatsApp, the writer does not produce a generic response to the topic. They produce a response to the exact question asked, mapped against the specific Learning Outcomes for that unit, at the cognitive depth required by the command verbs in the task.

For students who are unsure of their unit's exact requirements, we review the CMI unit specification alongside the assignment task. The writer identifies which Assessment Criteria each section of the assignment must address and structures the document accordingly.

Assignments require postgraduate-level critical analysis, original evaluation of strategic decisions, and engagement with the most demanding command verbs: Critique, Evaluate, and Justify at advanced depth.
